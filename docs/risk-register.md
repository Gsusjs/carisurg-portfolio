# Healthcare AI Risk Management and Algorithmic Equity Assessment

This risk register identifies the principal AI-technical, operational, ethical and equity risks associated with deploying an AI-assisted emergency department triage system across the Saint Cedric public hospital network. Each risk includes its likelihood, impact, mitigation strategy and measurable signal of success.

| Risk Name | Category | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy | Signal of Success |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Training-distribution shift between Mercer urban data and rural/outbreak cohorts** | AI-Technical | High | High | Clinical AI Lead monitors model drift weekly and validates quarterly against holdout datasets. A recall reduction greater than 3 percentage points triggers model review and suspension until revalidated. | Recall remains within ±3 percentage points (pp) across all sites. |
| **Model performance drift over time due to changing disease patterns** | AI-Technical | Medium | High | Continuous monitoring with scheduled annual retraining. Performance falling below the validation baseline triggers immediate model review and retraining. | AUROC and sensitivity remain above validation baseline. |
| **Alert fatigue leading to clinician dismissal of critical notifications** | Operational | High | High | Clinical Operations Lead reviews alert thresholds monthly. A clinical action rate below 15% triggers a mandatory redesign of the alert interface. | More than 15% of alerts result in documented clinical action. |
| **Workflow disruption increasing emergency department waiting times** | Operational | Medium | Medium | Phased pilot deployment with monthly workflow audits and direct EHR integration to identify bottlenecks. | Median triage time does not increase following deployment. |
| **Automation bias resulting in displacement of clinical judgement** | Ethical | High | High | Mandatory human approval for all recommendations. Structured simulation training every six months reinforces independent clinical decision-making. | ≥90% correct override rate during faulty AI simulation exercises. |
| **Limited explainability reducing trust and accountability** | Ethical | Medium | High | Use interpretable models where clinically appropriate or provide feature-level explanations (e.g., SHAP) for more complex models. | ≥80% of clinicians successfully explain AI rationale during usability audits. |
| **Differential under-triage of vulnerable groups (elderly, rural populations)** | Equity | High | High | Equity Lead conducts fairness audits disaggregated by age, sex and geography before every rollout. Deployment is withheld if subgroup performance gaps exceed 5 pp. | Recall gap between demographic groups remains below 5 pp. |
| **Patient data breach or cybersecurity compromise** | Ethical / Operational | Medium | High | Enforce encryption, multi-factor authentication, role-based access controls and quarterly penetration testing according to national health information governance requirements. | Zero reportable patient data breaches. |
| **Accountability gap following AI-related adverse events** | Ethical | Medium | High | Hospital Board assigns a named Clinical AI Owner responsible for reviewing all AI-related incidents and near-misses within 72 hours. | 100% of incidents receive formal review and documented corrective actions. |
| **Unsupported AI recommendations outside validated clinical pathways** | AI-Technical | Medium | High | Clinical Lead restricts outputs to validated clinical pathways. Recommendations outside approved pathways require mandatory senior clinician sign-off. | 100% of unsupported recommendations intercepted before implementation. |
